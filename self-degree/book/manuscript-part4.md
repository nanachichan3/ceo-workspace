# CHAPTER 7: Build Your Tribe

*Three months into my self-degree, I was alone in a room in Ansbach with a laptop and a list of things I wanted to learn. I had direction. I had resources. I had a vague plan. What I didn't have was anyone to talk to about any of it.*

*My friends back home were in university. They talked about exams and professors and dormitories. I couldn't explain what I was doing because I'd barely figured out how to explain it to myself. I felt like I was building something in the dark, without anyone to tell me if the walls were straight.*

*That loneliness almost broke me. Not because the learning was hard—because no one knew I was doing it.*

This is the part of the self-degree that nobody talks about. Everyone focuses on the resources, the roadmap, the credentials. But there is a hidden cost to learning on your own: the silence.

Most people who start a self-degree journey quit not because the material is too hard, but because nobody around them understands what they're doing. When you're studying alone, every small win goes uncelebrated. Every failure feels like proof that you made a mistake. Every moment of doubt has no one to push back against it.

You need people. Not just any people—people who are on the same path, or who have walked it before. This chapter is about finding them.

## The Loneliness of the Self-Directed Learner

There is a particular kind of isolation that comes with choosing an unconventional path. Your friends are doing what everyone else is doing—going to class, complaining about tuition, networking at career fairs. You are doing something nobody around you has a frame of reference for.

This isolation is dangerous. It makes you doubt yourself in ways that are hard to counter. When everyone around you is following a script and you are not, the default assumption is that you are the one who is wrong. Not the system. You.

I nearly quit dozens of times in those first months. Not because I was failing—but because I couldn't tell if I was succeeding. There were no grades, no professors, no deadlines. Just me, a list of topics, and the uncomfortable question: how do I know if this is working?

The answer, I eventually learned, is: you can't know alone. You need other people to reflect your progress back to you. You need witnesses.

## Why Community Is Infrastructure

Here is a reframe that changed how I think about finding your learning community: **it is not a nice-to-have. It is infrastructure.**

Just as you need electricity and internet to run a business, you need community to sustain a self-degree. The right community does several things at once:

**It validates your path.** When someone else is doing what you're doing—or has done it—you stop feeling like an outlier. The self-degree stops feeling like a reckless gamble and starts feeling like a legitimate choice. This alone can be enough to keep you going through the doubt-filled early months.

**It provides feedback.** Learning alone means you don't know what you don't know. A good community—peers at the same level, mentors who have been further—gives you signal on where you actually stand. Are you progressing faster or slower than expected? Are there gaps in your knowledge you can't see? Community answers these questions.

**It creates accountability.** This one surprised me. I assumed accountability required authority—someone with the power to reward or punish. But real accountability, the kind that changes behavior, comes from identity. When the people around you know what you're trying to do, and you care about their opinion, the social pressure to keep your commitments becomes enormous.

**It multiplies resources.** The best community isn't just people who support you—it's people who bring their own resources, connections, and knowledge. A network of serious learners is a resource amplifier. Someone knows a book you haven't read. Someone has solved the problem you're stuck on. Someone knows a mentor who might take a meeting. You access all of it through the network.

## Where to Find Your People

The traditional path comes with a built-in community: your classmates, your professors, your campus organizations. You don't have to look for it—it arrives with your enrollment.

The self-degree offers no such convenience. You have to build the community yourself. Here is where to start:

**Online communities.** This is where most self-directed learners find their first tribe. Reddit communities like r/learnprogramming, r/selfhosted, r/Sheetmetal and niche communities around specific disciplines have deep expertise and active discussion. Twitter (or Bluesky) #100DaysOfCode culture created entire cohorts of people learning to code together, publicly, with mutual support. Discord servers exist for nearly every field. Find the ones with active moderation and a culture of helping beginners.

The key is not to lurk. Lurk for a week to understand the culture. Then post. Ask questions. Share what you're learning. The people who contribute become the tribe. The people who only consume never find belonging.

**Local meetups and co-working spaces.** The internet is great for knowledge. It is terrible for human connection. Find the equivalent of what universities provide by looking locally: co-working spaces with community events, language exchange meetups, maker spaces, hackathons, reading groups, Toastmasters chapters. These exist in most cities. They require you to show up in person, which is uncomfortable, which is exactly why most people don't do it—which means the competition for genuine connection is lower.

**Master's degrees, short.** Not a full degree—but structured group learning experiences. Intensive weekend workshops, month-long bootcamps, cohort-based online courses (CBOCs). These bundle you with a group of people who are all investing time and money in the same skill, at the same time. The relationships formed in those compressed timeframes are often deeper than what happens in four years of university, because the shared intensity creates bonds fast. If you can afford even one structured experience in your target field, the network you build there may outlast everything else.

**The mentorship network.** One mentor is worth more than a hundred peers. But finding a mentor requires you to be worth mentoring—and that means demonstrating that you're serious before anyone will invest in you. Share what you're learning. Show your work. Ask specific questions, not vague ones. The fastest path to a mentor is to become the kind of person a mentor wants to find.

## The Geometry of Your Network

Not all connections are equal. Think of your learning network as having three concentric rings:

**The inner ring: accountability partners.** One or two people who are at the same stage as you, doing the same kind of work. You check in with each other daily or weekly. You share wins and setbacks. You celebrate each other's progress and call each other out when you're slacking. This is the highest-intensity relationship in your network, and you need at least one person in this ring.

**The middle ring: peer community.** A group of ten to fifty people who are on a similar path, at various stages. You're not in daily contact, but you exist in the same space. You post updates, share resources, give feedback on each other's work. This is the group that normalizes what you're doing—and makes you feel less alone.

**The outer ring: mentors and experts.** People who have already done what you're trying to do. You're not in regular contact, but you know who they are, you've learned from their public work, and you've reached out to at least a few of them directly. Most self-learners build only the outer ring (follow famous people online) and neglect the inner two. This is a mistake. The inner rings are where the actual support lives.

## Learning in Public

There is one technique that has transformed more self-degree journeys than any other, and it costs nothing: learning in public.

This doesn't mean performing. It means documenting your process as you go—sharing what you're learning, what you're struggling with, what you just figured out. A blog post. A Twitter thread. A YouTube vlog. A GitHub repository with a detailed README. Updates in a Discord community.

When you learn in public, several things happen that don't happen when you learn alone:

First, you are forced to articulate what you're learning—and articulation reveals gaps. If you can't explain something clearly enough to write it down, you don't understand it as well as you thought. The act of writing is also the act of testing your understanding.

Second, you attract your people. The internet is very good at connecting people who are interested in the same things. When you post about learning machine learning, other people learning machine learning find you. When you write about your homeschool journey, other homeschool families find you. Over time, the audience becomes a community.

Third, you build a public record of your progress. This matters more than most people realize. When you apply for a job, when you want a mentor to take you seriously, when you need to prove to yourself how far you've come—the public record is irrefutable evidence. A sequence of blog posts showing six months of learning is more convincing than a transcript from a university where you were passively present.

Fourth, you give back before you're ready. Most people wait until they are expert enough to help others. Learning-in-public allows you to share your struggles and discoveries at every stage. The beginner who writes honestly about what they learned today is often more helpful to other beginners than the expert who writes with assumed knowledge.

Start today. It doesn't matter if no one reads it yet. Write it anyway. The act of recording your learning is the point, not the audience.

## The Tribe You Build

Here is what most people miss about community: **you are not just looking for a tribe. You are building one.**

Every person who has ever found a learning community was once looking for one, and then decided to stop waiting and start creating what they were looking for. They organized the first meetup. They started the Discord server. They posted the first discussion thread.

You don't need permission. You don't need to be an expert. You just need to be the person who starts.

This is what the self-degree ultimately teaches you: you are not waiting for the system to create the conditions for your success. You are building the conditions yourself, piece by piece, with the people you gather along the way.

And the people you gather will be some of the most valuable assets you ever collect.

# CHAPTER 8: The World Has Changed. Now What?

Eight chapters ago, I told you a story about a cold train to Berlin and a choice that made no sense to anyone except me. A scholarship declined. Four years of learning without a map. And a question I couldn't stop asking: if I could build this myself, why can't everyone?

The honest answer is: they can. Most of them just don't know it yet.

This book exists because I spent four years learning on my own terms and found that the path not only works—it works better than the alternative for a growing number of people, in a growing number of fields, at a fraction of the cost and time. And because the world has finally caught up to the point where that choice is available to anyone with a laptop and the discipline to use it.

The 2 Sigma Problem was the theoretical proof. The self-degree framework is the practical one. And the hundreds of stories in this book—the dropouts who built careers, the career changers who skipped the master's degree, the teenagers who built billion-dollar companies, the professionals who proved that credentials are not the ceiling—they are the evidence.

But evidence is not enough. You have to choose.

## The Choice No One Else Can Make For You

I want to be direct with you here, because this book has been building toward this moment.

Choosing the self-degree is not the obvious choice. Most people around you—your parents, your teachers, your career counselors—will not understand it. They are not wrong, exactly. The conventional path still works for many people. A university degree is still valuable in many fields. There are good reasons to go.

But there are also good reasons not to go. And those reasons matter more today than they did ten years ago.

If you already know what you want to learn, and you know how to learn it, and the credentials don't matter in your target field—then the conventional path is not a shortcut. It's a detour.

If you are a parent reading this, wondering whether your child needs school to succeed—consider this: the school system was designed for a world that no longer exists. It was built to produce workers for an industrial economy that has been automated away. Your child is being prepared for a future that nobody has fully described yet. What they need is not more of the same. They need to learn how to learn, how to adapt, and how to find their own direction. That is not what school teaches. It is what the self-degree is designed to teach.

If you are a young person, someone in their twenties or thirties, someone who looked at the conventional path and felt the walls closing in—you already know something is wrong. You just need permission to act on it. This book is your permission.

## The Real Risk

Here is the question I get asked more than any other: isn't the self-degree riskier than a degree?

The answer is: it depends on what you're optimizing for.

A degree trades your time, your money, and your freedom for a credential, a network, and a structured learning experience. It reduces the ambiguity of the self-degree—but it also reduces your control. You learn what the curriculum dictates, at the pace the institution sets, in the format the professor chooses. For some people, that structure is exactly what they need. For others, it is a cage.

The self-degree takes ambiguity and turns it into freedom. You choose your direction, your resources, your pace. You build your network from scratch. You create your own credentials. The risk is real: you might pick the wrong direction, use bad resources, build a weak network, or lack the discipline to follow through. These are genuine risks.

But here is what the data shows: the people who succeed at the self-degree don't succeed because they took less risk. They succeed because they took the risk seriously. They built systems. They found accountability. They learned in public. They found mentors. They treated the self-degree with the same rigor that the best students bring to a university.

The difference between success and failure in self-degree is almost never intelligence or resources. It is discipline, community, and the willingness to iterate when something isn't working.

## The Question That Matters

I want to close with the question I wish someone had asked me on that night train to Berlin.

Not "are you sure?" — because certainty is not available to anyone making a big choice. Not "what if you fail?" — because failure is always possible, and the self-degree includes more tools for recovery than most alternatives.

The question that matters is this:

**What do you actually want from your education?**

Not what your parents want. Not what society expects. Not what will make you feel safe. What do *you* want?

Do you want to understand something deeply? Do you want to build something that didn't exist before? Do you want to work on problems that matter to you, with people who inspire you? Do you want freedom over structure, mastery over credentials, real learning over the performance of learning?

If what you want is a credential, get a credential. If what you want is an education—the kind that actually changes how you think, what you can do, and who you become—then the self-degree might be the path.

It was for me. Not one day of regret. Not one hour.

And if you decide to walk this path — I am not asking you to walk it alone. That is the whole point of everything in this book. Build your pillars. Find your accountability partner. Hire your mentor when you need one. Learn in public and let people find you. Build your tribe.

The institutions will not disappear. Universities are remarkable places with irreplaceable things to offer. They always will be. But the monopoly is over. The bundle has been unbundled. And for the first time in history, you can choose exactly the education you want—and build it yourself.

The world has changed.

The question is no longer whether alternatives exist. The question is whether you are ready to build yours.
